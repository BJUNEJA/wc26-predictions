Every other row is a challenger: a different model, a different method, or a different source of intelligence. All scored on the same Brier scale, on the same matches. The question the leaderboard answers: does any signal, from any source, forecast better than the Elo ensemble?

Brier is the primary metric (lower is better; 0 = clairvoyant, 0.667 = coin flip). Skill measures improvement over the naive base-rate forecaster (positive = the model adds real information). SE of Brier ≈ 0.05: models within ~0.05 of each other are statistically indistinguishable at this sample size. The leaderboard becomes evidence, not entertainment, only as N grows.

Dynamic trust allocation (multi-armed bandit)

The "Trust" column shows each model's probability of being the best forecaster, estimated via softmax over inverse Brier scores (a Thompson sampling approximation). Unlike a fixed champion that holds its throne until a challenger formally dethrones it, trust allocation shifts continuously as evidence accumulates. A model that starts cold (high Brier) can earn trust back by performing well on recent matches; a leader can lose ground by missing a sequence. This is the multi-armed bandit formulation of model selection: each "arm" is a forecasting model, each match is a pull, and the reward is the inverse of the Brier penalty. The allocation answers the question: if you had to bet on one model for the next match, which one would you choose, and with what confidence?

The champion-challenger framework

In production analytics, a champion is the model currently making decisions; a challenger is any candidate replacement that runs in parallel, scored on the same outcomes, but not yet trusted with decisions. The champion holds its position until a challenger demonstrates statistically significant improvement over a sustained evaluation window, not a lucky streak. This framework is standard practice in credit scoring (OCC Bulletin 2011-12), clinical trials (where it maps onto non-inferiority designs), and recommendation systems (A/B testing). Its core discipline: the challenger must beat the champion on the champion's turf, with the champion's metrics, over the champion's evaluation period. Selection bias (submitting only your best backtest) and survivorship bias (dropping challengers that start poorly) are the two threats the framework exists to prevent.

How to enter a challenger

Any model, any method, any person, from any department. The tournament tests one question: can your method forecast better than the current leader? You submit predictions, not code. Your model runs on your machine; what enters the leaderboard is a CSV of probability triples.

Step 1: get the platform

Download the platform zip from Canvas (or ask the instructor). Unzip to a folder. You need Python 3.8+ with pandas and numpy.

Step 2: build your model

Copy templates/sample_model.py, rename it yourname_modelname.py, and replace the placeholder probabilities with your model's output. Your model reads data/results.csv (49,400+ internationals) and predicts the 72 group matches in data/predictions_2026.csv. Use any method: regression, random forest, neural network, Bayesian, heuristic, or informed guesswork. External data (FIFA rankings, market odds, squad values) is permitted but must be documented.

Step 3: submit

One command:

python python/submit_challenger.py yourname_modelname "Your Full Name" "One-line method" your_predictions.csv

Example: python python/submit_challenger.py jsmith_xgboost "Jane Smith" "XGBoost on Elo + FIFA ranking features" jsmith_predictions.csv

The script validates your CSV (correct columns, team names, probabilities summing to 1), registers your model, and submits all predictions. Share the output with the instructor for the next site build.

Tournament rules

Rule 1Before kickoff. Predictions for any match must be submitted before that match begins. The timestamp is the audit trail.
Rule 2No look-ahead. Your model must not use information from after the prediction date. Using Tuesday's results to predict Monday's match is disqualification.
Rule 3Full probability vector. Submit p_home, p_draw, p_away for each match, summing to 1.0. A pick alone (without calibrated probabilities) cannot be scored on Brier.
Rule 4No cherry-picking. Submit all remaining unplayed matches. You may not submit only the matches you feel confident about; that inflates your record against models that predict everything.
Rule 5One model per person. You may update your model (re-submit with --force) but you may not run multiple models and keep only the best. That is selection bias, and it is the forecasting equivalent of p-hacking.
Rule 6Same base data. All models use data/results.csv. External data is permitted (betting odds, injuries, weather) but must be documented in your method description.
Rule 7Reproducibility. Retain your code. You may be asked to demonstrate that your model produces the submitted predictions from the stated inputs.
ViolationFirst offense: warning and notation on the leaderboard. Second offense: removal from the tournament. The instructor has final authority on disputes.
